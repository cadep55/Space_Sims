Space Sims: 2D Spacecraft Transfer Orbits

by Cade Patterson

Hello and welcome! I am an extremely visual learner, so for 2026 I’m endeavoring to combine the space systems engineering knowledge I’m developing at Johns Hopkins University with AI and applied mathematics to create a series of simple, interactive mini-projects.

The goal of these models is to give students and space enthusiasts a fast, intuitive way to visualize Hohmann transfers — both for single-body planetary rendezvous and heliocentric interplanetary transfers.

These simulations are intentionally 2D and currently do not consider orbital inclination, plane changes, or full multi-body dynamics. Expanding these models while keeping them intuitive is a future goal.

Notes, equations, and reference parameters are primarily taken from Space Mission Engineering: The New SMAD and orbital-mechanics.space .

Check out the simulations below, test the math, and feel free to reach out with ideas, improvements, or bug fixes you’d like to see next.

How to Use the Simulations

1) Select the Orbits or Planets

  • In the single-body rendezvous model, choose the initial and target orbits (defined by periapsis and apoapsis).
  • In the interplanetary model, select a starting planet and a target planet. The heliocentric model currently includes planets from Mercury through Jupiter, with a visual band representing the asteroid belt.

2) Set the Initial Phase Angles

  • Phase angle defines where each orbiting body is at simulation start.
  • Changing the phase does not change orbit shape — only the timing and geometry of the transfer.

3) Use Auto-Rendezvous (Highly Recommended)

Auto-Rendezvous computes the correct initial phase angle so that the spacecraft arrives at the target orbit or planet at the same time and location.
  • This function adjusts only timing, not orbit geometry.
  • If Auto-Rendezvous is not used, the spacecraft will still perform the transfer but may visibly miss the target.
  • This highlights a key real-world concept: correct ΔV alone does not guarantee rendezvous — phasing matters.

4) Recommended Transfers

For best results, try transferring from periapsis to apoapsis or apoapsis to periapsis. These configurations align naturally with Hohmann transfer geometry.

5) Interpreting the Output Data

  • The bottom data panel reports ΔV for each burn and the total ΔV.
  • Total ΔV is driven purely by orbital mechanics — spacecraft mass does not change ΔV, but does affect propellant usage via the rocket equation.
  • Time of flight is shown in days and is fixed for a Hohmann transfer between the selected orbits.

6) Controlling Time and Visualization

  • Increase the time scale to speed up the simulation.
  • Use zoom, orbit spacing, and pan controls to better visualize the solar system.
  • The spacecraft trace shows the path over time and intentionally breaks if a jump would occur due to transfer completion.
